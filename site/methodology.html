Methodology

How we select, evaluate, and score transformative AI scenarios to gauge global preparedness.

Overview

AI Scenarios tracks published scenarios describing transformative AI futures and evaluates how prepared current policy frameworks are to address them. The goal is to provide a transparent, data-driven assessment of global AI readiness — not to predict the future, but to measure our preparedness for a range of plausible futures.

Scenario Selection

Scenarios are drawn from published research, policy papers, and expert analyses. To be included, a scenario must:

  • Be published by a credible institution or recognized expert
  • Describe a specific, plausible pathway for transformative AI
  • Have clear implications for economic, social, or political systems
  • Be sufficiently detailed to evaluate against existing policy frameworks

Each scenario is analyzed using the STEEP framework (Social, Technological, Economic, Environmental, Political) to ensure comprehensive coverage of implications.

Evaluation Criteria

Each scenario is scored on three dimensions using a 1–5 scale. Likelihood is assessed by Windfall’s Expert Panel of economists and technologists. Scenario Impact is assessed through public input, grounding the Tracker in democratic legitimacy. Preparedness is maintained by Windfall and updated continuously as the policy landscape changes.

Likelihood (1–5)

How probable is this scenario? Assessed by the Expert Panel.

ScoreRatingMeaning
1ImprobableVery unlikely given current evidence
2PossiblePossible but requires several uncertain assumptions
3PlausibleCould go either way based on current trends
4ProbableLikely based on current trends
5EmergingAlready underway or near-certain

Scenario Impact (1–5)

What happens to the social contract if this scenario materialises with no policy response? Scored by Windfall based on public input (e.g. from Collective Intelligence Project).

ScoreRatingMeaning
1Systemic RiskInstitutions fail, cascading collapse
2Social Contract Broken for AllUniversal loss of trust, fairness, security
3Social Contract Broken for SomeSevere harm concentrated in specific groups
4Social Contract StableManageable disruption within existing capacity
5Social Contract ImprovingLives are improving for future generations

Preparedness (1–5)

Do sufficient policies exist and are they enacted? Scored by Windfall and updated continuously via Policy Atlas.

ScoreRatingMeaning
1Critically UnpreparedNo sufficient policies exist
2Highly UnpreparedSufficient policy ideas exist but are untested
3UnpreparedCredible policies exist but aren’t enacted
4Almost PreparedPolicies enacted to address some key problems
5PreparedSufficient policies enacted and operational

Overall Preparedness Score

The headline preparedness score is a weighted average of all scenario preparedness scores (1–5), weighted by each scenario’s likelihood score. This means more likely scenarios contribute more to the overall score, reflecting the principle that we should be most prepared for the futures most likely to occur.

Formula: Overall = Σ(preparednessi × likelihoodi) / Σ(likelihoodi)

Net Impact

The Net Impact score combines Impact and Preparedness into a single measure. Higher preparedness mitigates worse impact scores, reflecting the principle that good policy can reduce the harm of adverse scenarios.

Formula: Net Impact = impact + ((preparedness − 1) / 4) × (5 − impact)

The result is on a 1–5 scale: 1 means systemic risk with no preparation, 5 means improving outcomes with full preparedness. Each scenario decomposes into policy problem statements (e.g. Unemployment, Fiscal Crisis, Extreme Inequality, Existential Risk, Democratic Erosion). These problems are then mapped to solutions in the Policy Atlas. When new solutions emerge, boosts to Preparedness flow upwards through the system, updating the charts in real time.

Policy Challenges

Each scenario is tagged with one or more policy challenge categories that it raises. These tags connect scenarios to relevant policy proposals:

  • Mass Unemployment — Widespread job displacement
  • Fiscal Crisis — Tax base erosion and fiscal instability
  • Inequality — Wealth and income concentration
  • AI Concentration — Market power concentration in AI firms
  • Skills Obsolescence — Rapid depreciation of human skills
  • Public Service Disruption — Government service transformation challenges
  • Democratic Erosion — Threats to democratic governance
  • Existential Risk — Catastrophic or existential-level threats

Data Pipeline

All data is stored in structured CSV files (scenarios, evaluations, policies) and compiled into chart-ready JSON using a Node.js build script. The entire dataset and methodology are open-source and available for inspection on GitHub.

Limitations

  • Evaluations reflect informed judgment, not precise scientific measurement
  • Scenario coverage is not exhaustive — important futures may be missing
  • Preparedness assessments focus primarily on economic and governance dimensions
  • Scores are relative assessments and should not be compared across different frameworks
